Language in Later Life on Tyneside

The Language in Later Life on Tyneside project (LiLLT) was established by Dr Heike Pichler (Newcastle University, UK) in order to explore the everyday language use of adults aged 70+ in Tyneside, north-east England. We work to document, explain and celebrate the diversity of older Tyneside adults’ accents and dialects through our research, teaching and public engagement activities.

Learn More

The key objectives of our activities are to explain:

Why older Tyneside adults do not all speak the same.

Do they pronounce words differently (e.g., walking vs. walkin’) because they had different educational opportunities or career trajectories in early and mid-life? Do they choose different words (e.g., he was very strict vs. he was dead strict) because they experience later life in different ways – for example, some feeling older and others younger than they actually are?

Whether and why some older Tyneside adults change the way they speak over time.

Do they change their choice of grammatical structures (e.g., I got spoilt vs. I was spoilt); and if so, is this because of changes in their social worlds – for example, who they regularly communicate with and how often? Do they change their pronunciations (e.g. better vs. be’er); and if so, is this because their muscles gradually weaken as they get older?

To answer these questions and create a lasting record of the traditional Tyneside dialect, we have interviewed over fifty Tyneside adults aged 70+ about their experiences of growing up and growing old in post-war and post-millennial Tyneside. The recorded interviews form the basis of our linguistic analyses; the lived experiences shared by project participants help us interpret our findings.

The project develops a better understanding of how age and ageing affect human behaviours such as language use, and champions older adults as creative and entertaining language users.
